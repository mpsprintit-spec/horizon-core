# Pragmatics Module

## Overview

The pragmatics module documents Indonesian language use in context: register, politeness, social distance, cultural expectations, intent, and implicature. It helps Horizon interpret what a person means, not only what words literally encode.

## Register levels

- **Formal:** suitable for institutions, public services, official writing, and respectful first contact. Examples: `Apakah Anda memerlukan bantuan?`, `Terima kasih atas informasinya.`
- **Neutral:** broadly appropriate across everyday settings. Examples: `Saya bisa membantu`, `Tolong kirim datanya.`
- **Informal:** suitable among friends, family, or close peers. Examples: `Aku bantu ya`, `Kamu sudah makan?`
- **Colloquial:** highly casual and context-dependent. Examples: `gak`, `nggak`, `dong`, `kok`, `nih`.

## Politeness strategies

- Use `tolong` for requests that impose on another person: `Tolong tunggu sebentar.`
- Use `mohon` for formal or institutional requests: `Mohon lengkapi formulir ini.`
- Use `silakan` to invite or permit politely: `Silakan masuk.`
- Use titles or kin terms such as `Bapak`, `Ibu`, `Kak`, or `Mas/Mbak` when culturally appropriate.
- Avoid overly intimate pronouns (`aku`, `kamu`) in formal service contexts unless the relationship supports them.

## Social and cultural context

Indonesian often favors indirectness when refusing, correcting, or requesting. A response such as `Mungkin nanti` (“Maybe later”) may function as a soft refusal depending on tone and situation. Corrections are often softened with `maaf`, `sepertinya`, or `mungkin`.

## Communication intent

Common intents include:

- requesting help: `Bisa bantu saya?`
- giving permission: `Silakan digunakan.`
- warning: `Hati-hati, lantainya licin.`
- confirming: `Benar, datanya sudah masuk.`
- declining politely: `Maaf, saya belum bisa.`
- reassuring: `Tidak apa-apa, kita periksa lagi.`

## Safety and trust

For safety-sensitive contexts, prefer clear, non-ambiguous wording and avoid colloquial shortcuts. `Berhenti sekarang` is clearer than `stop dulu ya` in urgent safety contexts. This aligns with Horizon's priority that human safety comes before speed or style.
